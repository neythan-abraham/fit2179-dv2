Conclusion · What Can Be Done?

The Crisis Is Real — But So Is the Hope

Australia holds an extraordinary responsibility. Home to some of the world's most unique and ancient species — marsupials that evolved in isolation for millions of years — it has also presided over the highest rate of mammal extinction on Earth. Since European settlement, 39 mammal species have vanished. Over 2,200 species are currently listed as threatened. The data is unambiguous: without urgent intervention, this crisis will deepen.

But the data also shows us exactly where to act.

🌿

Protect critical habitat

New South Wales (760 species) and Western Australia (614 species) bear the heaviest burden. Expanding national parks, enforcing land-clearing laws, and creating wildlife corridors between fragmented habitats in these states would have the greatest measurable impact on slowing species loss across all taxonomic groups.

🦊

Control invasive predators

Feral cats kill an estimated 1.4 billion native animals annually in Australia. Predators are the leading cause of mammal extinction in our data. Targeted baiting programs, predator-free fenced sanctuaries, and the national feral cat management strategy have already demonstrated success — with Bilby and Eastern Bettong populations recovering in protected zones.

🌡️

Act on climate change

The Bramble Cay Melomys became the first mammal driven extinct by climate change in 2016. The Mountain Pygmy Possum faces the same fate as alpine snowfields shrink. Climate change is accelerating every other threat. Without deep emissions cuts and climate-resilient habitat management, even successful predator control programs will be undermined.

💊

Fund recovery programs

The Northern Hairy-nosed Wombat — once down to just 35 individuals — now numbers over 300 thanks to a dedicated recovery program. The Tasmanian Devil Facial Tumour Disease program has established insurance populations across mainland Australia. Targeted, well-funded recovery plans work. Australia needs to scale them up from dozens of species to hundreds.

Every species lost is an irreversible loss — not just of biodiversity, but of millions of years of evolution.

The tools exist. The knowledge exists. What remains is the political will and public commitment to act before more squares on that grid go dark forever.
